Virat Kohli and the Discipline of Showing Up

Cricket · Mindset · Inspiration

I never planned on writing about cricket on my personal website. But some people influence you beyond their field, and Virat Kohli is one of those people for me. What inspires me about Kohli isn’t the trophies or the numbers everyone already knows. It’s the discipline behind them. The kind that shows up quietly, daily, without needing applause. There was a time when his form dipped. For someone who had spent years dominating, that phase felt uncomfortable to watch. The noise got louder, opinions got sharper, and expectations didn’t ease up. What stood out to me was how he responded not with excuses, not with shortcuts, but with consistency.

In my own work—building systems, writing code, solving problems—there are days when progress feels invisible. Things break. Solutions don’t come instantly. Motivation drops. Earlier, I used to wait for the “right mood” to work properly. Now I know better. Kohli’s journey taught me that professionals don’t wait for motivation; they rely on discipline. You sit down and do the work. You fix what’s broken. You improve one small thing at a time. Not because it’s exciting, but because it’s necessary.

That’s the quiet inspiration Virat Kohli gave me. And it’s one I try to live by, every single day.

Mumbai: Chaos and Dreams

City · Life · Growth

I was born and brought up in Mumbai. So when I talk about this city, it’s not from the outside. It’s from living it—every single day. Mumbai isn’t easy. Anyone who says otherwise hasn’t spent enough time here.
There’s traffic that drains your energy. Local trains that push personal space to its limits. Days that feel rushed, loud, and unforgiving.
That’s the side people usually notice first.
But Mumbai is also exactly what you choose to take from it. If you come here expecting comfort, it will disappoint you. If you come here ready to work, it will slowly open doors.

Yes, there’s chaos. Yes, things are overcrowded. And yes, many bad things happen—not because the people are bad, but because systems fail, governance fails, and ordinary people end up paying the price. That part is real, and it shouldn’t be ignored.
Still, reducing Mumbai to only its problems misses the bigger picture.
This city teaches you patience without lectures. Strength without motivation speeches. Discipline without rules being spelled out.
What stays with me is the quiet resilience of the people. The office worker standing in a packed train every morning. The street vendor who shows up daily no matter the weather. The student chasing a future in a city that never slows down. There’s something grounding about growing up in a place like this. It makes you realistic. It makes you independent. It teaches you that progress comes from consistency, not comfort.
I’ve had frustrating days here. I’ve complained about the traffic, the crowd, the noise—just like everyone else. But when I think honestly about where I’d rather be, there’s no debate.
I wouldn’t choose any other place.
Not because Mumbai is perfect—but because it’s real. It gives back what you put into it. And if you stay long enough, it shapes you into someone stronger than you were before.
That’s the Mumbai I know. And that’s home.

Living and working here shaped my understanding of systems. In many ways, Mumbai itself is a distributed system — chaotic on the surface, but remarkably resilient underneath.

Politics, Power, and Responsibility

Politics · Society · Thought

Politics today is louder than ever, yet clarity feels rare. In a digital world, influence spreads faster than responsibility.

What concerns me is not disagreement, but misinformation. Technology amplifies voices, but it also amplifies consequences. With reach must come accountability.

Whether in leadership, governance, or systems design, the principle remains the same — power without responsibility eventually fails.
